<div n="lb"/>{#वाक्यं, अवयवोपेतवाक्यं, आनुपूर्व्वं#}. A syllogism has five mem-
<div n="lb"/>bers or {#अवयवाः,#} according to the Nyāya system, viz.
<div n="lb"/>1. The proposition or assertion to be proved, {#प्रतिज्ञा;#} 2. the
<div n="lb"/>reason given, {#हेतुः;#} 3. the example or illustration, {#उदाहरणं;#}
<div n="lb"/>4. the application, {#उपनयः;#} 5. the conclusion, {#निगमनं#}. Thus,
<div n="lb"/>1. ‘The hill is fiery,’ {#पर्व्वतो वह्निमान्#}. 2. ‘Because it smokes,’
<div n="lb"/>{#धूमवत्त्वात्#}. 3. ‘Whatever smokes is fiery, like a culinary
<div n="lb"/>hearth,’ {#यो यो धूमवान् स वह्निमान् यथा महानसः#}. 4. ‘This (hill)
<div n="lb"/>has smoke which is always accompanied with fire,’ {#वह्नि-#}
<div n="lb"/>{#व्याप्यधूमवान् अयं#}. 5. ‘Therefore it is fiery,’ {#तस्माद् वह्निमान्#}.
<div n="lb"/>According to the Mīmānsā school, a syllogism has only
<div n="lb"/>the last three members.
